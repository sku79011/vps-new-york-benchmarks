## Why New York? The Geography Argument Is Actually Physics

Here's the thing people skip over when picking a VPS: latency isn't just a configuration choice. It's physics. Data travels at the speed of light, and light takes time to travel distance. You can tune your server all you want — if it's sitting on the wrong continent, you're fighting the laws of the universe.

New York sits at one of the densest internet exchange points on the planet. The NYIIX (New York International Internet Exchange), combined with private peering arrangements from major carriers like DE-CIX, Cloudflare, and Google, means traffic leaving an NYC datacenter gets handed off to the right networks fast. We're talking 5–30ms latency to the entire US Eastern Seaboard, and 70–90ms to London and Amsterdam — the latter being possible because transatlantic submarine cables happen to land near New York.

For context: if 40% of users abandon a site that takes more than 3 seconds to load, and Amazon found that every 100ms delay drops sales by 1%, then the question isn't whether server location matters. It's how much money you're leaving on the table by ignoring it.

New York also happens to be the financial capital of the world. NYSE, NASDAQ, major FX brokers — they all live here. For algorithmic traders and people running automated bots on MT4/MT5, a VPS New York setup isn't optional. It's infrastructure.

## Who Actually Needs a VPS in New York?

Not everyone does. Let's be honest about this.

**It makes a lot of sense for:**

- **East Coast US businesses** — e-commerce, SaaS, anything where your users are concentrated in New York, Boston, Washington D.C., or anywhere along the I-95 corridor. Sub-10ms response times for those users changes how your app feels.
- **Forex and stock traders** — running automated strategies that need to be physically close to broker servers. Scalpers especially can't afford 80ms round trips from a home connection.
- **Discord bots and web scrapers** — apps that run 24/7 and benefit from stable, fast connections to US-based APIs and services.
- **Developers and students** — hosting portfolios, side projects, learning environments. New York is a solid default if you're building for a US audience.
- **Anyone with transatlantic needs** — if you're serving both North American and European users, NYC is legitimately the best single-node compromise you can make.

**It matters less if** your users are on the US West Coast (LA or Seattle nodes would serve them better), or predominantly in Asia (in which case, Hong Kong or Tokyo start making more sense).
